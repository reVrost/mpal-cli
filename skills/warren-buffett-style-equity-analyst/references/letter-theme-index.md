# Letter Theme Index

Use this as a navigation guide for source work. Verify the underlying letter, annual report, or transcript before citing a specific year or claim.

## Core Berkshire Letter Themes

| Theme | Where To Look First | What To Extract |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Owner orientation | Owner's Manual; annual letters | Partnership mentality, long-term owner base, candid reporting. |
| Intrinsic value | Letters across decades; annual reports | Book value limits, per-share value growth, value range humility. |
| Owner earnings | Mid-1980s letters; annual reports | Maintenance capex, accounting earnings versus economic earnings. |
| Economic goodwill | Early letters on acquired businesses | Difference between accounting goodwill and durable earnings power. |
| Insurance float | Annual reports and insurance sections | Float cost, underwriting discipline, reserving, catastrophe risk. |
| Capital allocation | Annual letters; annual meeting Q&A | Reinvest, acquire, repurchase, repay debt, dividend, or hold cash. |
| Share repurchases | Annual letters and annual reports | Repurchase only below intrinsic value and with excess capital. |
| Acquisitions | Owner's Manual; annual letters | Simple criteria, fair dealing, managers staying in place, no auctions if avoidable. |
| Circle of competence | Annual meeting archive | Knowable businesses, humility, avoiding complexity. |
| Moats | Annual meeting archive; letters | Durable competitive advantage, pricing power, low-cost position, brand. |
| Management candor | Owner's Manual; letters | Reporting bad news, incentives, autonomy, trust. |
| Leverage risk | Letters and annual meeting Q&A | Avoiding debt that can force bad decisions. |
| Market cycles | Partnership letters; annual letters | Avoid forecasts, exploit emotional markets, measure over years. |
| Inflation | Annual meeting archive; historical letters | Pricing power, capital intensity, real returns. |
| Accounting quality | Annual reports; annual meeting Q&A | Adjusted earnings skepticism, pension/accounting estimates, look-through economics. |
| Opportunity cost | Annual meetings; letters | Compare any idea with the next-best use of capital. |
| Hurdle rates | Annual meetings; letters | Demand enough return to compensate for risk, taxes, liquidity, and uncertainty. |
| Temperament and inactivity | Annual meetings; letters | Patience, long holding periods, and willingness to do nothing. |
| Avoiding stupidity | Annual meetings; letters | Avoid leverage, promotion, overpayment, unknowable businesses, and needless complexity. |

## Suggested Year/Topic Workflows

### Business Quality Review

1. Start with Owner's Manual for owner orientation and acquisition philosophy.
2. Use annual letters for moat, management, and capital allocation examples.
3. Use annual meeting archive for topic-specific Q&A on the company's industry.
4. Apply the checklist to the current company using fresh filings.

### Intrinsic Value Review

1. Use letters discussing intrinsic value, owner earnings, and book value limitations.
2. Pull the company's current financials.
3. Normalize earnings and cash flow through a cycle.
4. Build conservative downside/base/upside value ranges.
5. State whether the margin of safety is enough.

### Insurance Or Financial Company Review

1. Use Berkshire annual reports for insurance float, underwriting profit/loss, reserves, and capital strength.
2. Use company filings for float/liability duration, credit quality, leverage, regulatory capital, and asset risk.
3. Separate accounting earnings from economic risk.

### Acquisition Review

1. Use Owner's Manual acquisition criteria.
2. Review target quality, price, management retention, capital needs, and integration risk.
3. Ask whether the acquisition improves per-share intrinsic value or only increases size.

### Portfolio Review

1. Separate core, compounders, cyclicals, financials, commodities, and speculation.
2. Identify positions outside the circle of competence.
3. Rank holdings by business quality, balance-sheet resilience, margin of safety, and opportunity cost.
4. Apply a hurdle-rate test against cash, an index, and the best existing holding.
5. Be willing to recommend doing nothing if taxes/friction and quality justify patience.

## Theme Notes

- Buffett-style analysis is not simply low P/E investing. Quality, durability, management, and reinvestment matter.
- Commodity businesses require extra caution unless there is a durable cost advantage or clear margin-of-safety asset value.
- Technology businesses are not automatically excluded, but fast change raises the burden of proof for moat durability and forecast confidence.
- Growth is valuable only when it can be funded sensibly and earn attractive incremental returns.
- Cash is a strategic asset when alternatives lack margin of safety.
