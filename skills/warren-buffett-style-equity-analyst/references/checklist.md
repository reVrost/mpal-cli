# Buffett-Style Analysis Checklist

Use this checklist for formal stock, portfolio, acquisition, or trim/avoid analysis.

## 1. Understandability

- What does the company sell?
- Who pays, how often, and why?
- What are the key variables that drive revenue, margins, and cash flow?
- Can a reasonably informed investor explain the business in two minutes?
- What parts are outside the circle of competence?

## 2. Moat And Competitive Position

- What prevents customers from leaving or competitors from copying?
- Is the moat brand, cost, scale, network, switching cost, regulation, distribution, culture, or something else?
- Is the moat widening, stable, or shrinking?
- What evidence proves pricing power?
- What would permanently impair the moat?

## 3. Economics

- Revenue growth over 5-10 years.
- Gross margin, operating margin, and margin stability.
- ROIC/ROE and whether leverage inflates returns.
- Free cash flow and owner earnings.
- Maintenance capex versus growth capex.
- Working-capital intensity.
- Incremental returns on reinvested capital.
- Share count trend.

## 4. Management And Capital Allocation

- Are managers candid about problems?
- Are incentives tied to per-share value creation?
- Does management buy, sell, or issue shares rationally?
- Are acquisitions disciplined and value-creating?
- Are buybacks below intrinsic value?
- Is debt used conservatively?
- Does management chase size or per-share economics?

## 5. Balance Sheet And Permanent Capital Loss

- Net debt, maturities, interest coverage, liquidity, covenants.
- Pension, lease, litigation, regulatory, or off-balance-sheet exposures.
- Cyclical downside if earnings fall sharply.
- Dilution risk or refinancing risk.
- Survival without access to friendly capital markets.

## 6. Intrinsic Value

- Normalize earnings and owner earnings.
- Use conservative growth and margin assumptions.
- Value non-operating assets and liabilities separately.
- Build downside/base/upside ranges.
- Avoid false precision.
- Compare current price to conservative value.
- State the margin of safety explicitly.

## 7. Opportunity Cost

- Is this better than cash?
- Is it better than an index?
- Is it better than the user's existing best holding?
- What hurdle rate should this idea clear given risk, taxes, liquidity, and uncertainty?
- Would selling a weaker holding to own this improve the portfolio?
- What is the cost of waiting?
- Is doing nothing a rational answer?

## 8. Decision Labels

- **Attractive**: understandable, durable, well-managed, financially resilient, and priced below conservative value.
- **Watchlist**: good business or interesting price, but missing evidence or margin of safety.
- **Too Hard**: cannot underwrite durability, accounting, management, industry change, or downside.
- **Avoid**: poor business, weak balance sheet, bad incentives, no margin of safety, or high permanent-loss risk.

## 9. Trim Or Sell Review

- Has price outrun intrinsic value?
- Has business quality deteriorated?
- Is management allocating capital poorly?
- Is the balance sheet now fragile?
- Is there a better use of capital?
- Is the position oversized relative to confidence and downside?
- Are taxes/friction worth paying?
- Is the reason to sell emotional price movement or a real change in value?
