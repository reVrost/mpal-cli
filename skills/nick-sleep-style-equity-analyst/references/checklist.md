# Checklist

Use this checklist before giving a final view.

## Business Destination

- What could this company become in 5-10 years?
- Is the destination specific enough to test with future evidence?
- Does the company need heroic market-share or margin assumptions to justify the
  case?
- What single fact would most weaken the destination?

## Customer Proposition

- Is the product cheaper, better, faster, easier, more trusted, or more complete
  because the company is larger?
- Are customers showing loyalty, habit, or repeat behavior?
- Is customer value improving, or is growth coming from extraction, promotion,
  subsidy, or channel stuffing?

## Scale Economies Shared

- Does scale lower unit costs?
- Are some of those savings shared with customers?
- Does sharing scale benefits attract more customers and deepen the moat?
- Would a competitor need to lose money or accept inferior economics to match
  the proposition?

## Culture And Incentives

- Does management communicate like long-term owners?
- Is compensation aligned with per-share value and durable business quality?
- Does the company make decisions that hurt near-term optics to improve the
  long-term business?
- Are there warning signs of promotion, dilution, aggressive accounting, or
  agency costs?

## Reinvestment Runway

- How much capital can be reinvested at attractive returns?
- Are incremental economics improving, stable, or deteriorating?
- Is reported margin depressed by productive growth spend or by weak economics?
- What is the likely return on the next dollar invested?

## Financial Evidence

- Revenue growth and quality.
- Gross margin, operating margin, free cash flow, and cash conversion.
- Capital intensity and working capital.
- Balance sheet strength, debt maturities, interest burden, and dilution.
- Per-share progress, not just aggregate growth.
- Segment economics and disclosure quality.

## Valuation And Prospective Return

- What does the current price imply about growth, margins, and terminal value?
- What are conservative, base, and upside scenarios?
- Does the valuation allow mistakes, or does everything need to go right?
- Is the prospective return attractive enough versus simpler alternatives?

## Red Flag Pass

Mark any red flag clearly:

- Growth does not improve the customer value proposition.
- Scale is captured only by management or shareholders.
- Financial engineering is presented as compounding.
- Management is promotional or poorly aligned.
- The company is managed for quarterly earnings optics.
- Leverage limits long-term optionality.
- Demand depends on transient fashion or one-off events.
- Culture, service, or customer trust is deteriorating.
- Complexity prevents clear destination analysis.
- Valuation is too stretched for the likely destination.

## Final Classification

- Compounder: Destination is strong, evidence is high quality, reinvestment
  runway is long, culture is aligned, and valuation leaves adequate prospective
  return.
- Watchlist: Business may be excellent, but price, missing evidence, leverage,
  or uncertainty prevents a strong current view.
- Too Hard: The destination depends on too many unknowable variables or
  accounting/segment disclosure is too opaque.
- Avoid: Customer proposition, culture, balance sheet, valuation, or business
  quality fails the test.
